\begin{abstract}
Aligned large language models must refuse harmful instructions while remaining helpful on benign tasks.
Yet behavioral guardrails can fail under adversarial prompts without obvious capability collapse, which motivates asking whether safety and helpfulness rely on shared or separable structure inside the network.
We study this question under the Linear Representation Hypothesis.
From residual-stream activations and contrastive prompts, we estimate safety-aligned and utility-aligned geometry using difference-of-means directions and activation-aware SVD subspaces, pairing refusal-vector-style summaries with richer low-rank subspace analyses emphasized in prior safety work.
We apply the same joint safety-utility comparison across several instruction-tuned checkpoints spanning multiple model families, including Gemma, Llama, Qwen, and Phi variants.
Anchors are AdvBench (harmful), Alpaca (benign breadth), and HelpSteer (human helpfulness contrasts).
Layer by layer we report cosine similarity between DoM directions, plus subspace overlap and principal-angle summaries from actSVD.
Selected setups additionally combine these geometric statistics with activation ablations and refusal-focused evaluation so internal overlap can be read alongside behavioral change.
We summarize cross-model patterns, connect them to localized refusal and safety-subspace evidence in the literature, and discuss limitations and next steps toward causal interventions.
\end{abstract}
